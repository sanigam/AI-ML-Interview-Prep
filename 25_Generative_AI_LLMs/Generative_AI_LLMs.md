# Generative AI and Large Language Models

📺 **Video Lecture:** https://youtu.be/q728r276RA0


## Interview Anchor
- **Large Language Model (LLM):** Transformer-based neural network (100M-100B+ parameters) trained via next-token prediction on massive text corpora
- **Scaling Laws:** Empirical relationships showing loss decreases as power law with model size and training data (Chinchilla laws)
- **Emergent Abilities:** Qualitatively new capabilities appearing at large scales (few-shot learning, reasoning, instruction-following) not present in smaller models

## Key Concepts Overview

Large language models represent a paradigm shift in AI. Rather than supervised learning on task-specific data, modern LLMs use unsupervised pretraining on internet-scale text, followed by instruction tuning and alignment. This approach unexpectedly enables zero-shot and few-shot task solving—a phenomenon not observed in smaller models. Understanding what LLMs can and cannot do is crucial for interviews: (1) LLMs are probabilistic models sampling from a learned distribution, not symbolic reasoners—they can hallucinate. (2) Scaling laws predict performance before training, guiding resource allocation. (3) Alignment techniques (RLHF, DPO) are critical for safety and usability. (4) Context windows, tokenization, and sampling strategies have dramatic practical impacts on quality. (5) Recent trends (instruction tuning, constitutional AI) reshape what's possible. This topic directly tests your understanding of modern AI: not just knowing transformer architecture, but understanding why scaling works, how to evaluate LLMs, and what makes them alignment-challenging.

### Q1: What exactly is a large language model? Define it precisely. What distinguishes LLMs from traditional NLP?

**A:** An LLM is a transformer-based neural network (typically decoder-only, autoregressive) with billions of parameters trained via next-token prediction on massive unlabeled text. Formally: given input tokens `x_1, ..., x_n`, the model learns `P(x_{n+1} | x_1:n)`, then extends autoregressively to predict sequences. Architecture: transformer decoder (no encoder), standard attention with causal masking. Training: optimize cross-entropy loss `Σ -log P(x_t | x_1:t-1)` on diverse internet text (books, web pages, code, etc.). Examples: GPT-2 (1.5B params), GPT-3 (175B), LLaMA (7B-65B), Claude, Gemini. What distinguishes from traditional NLP: (1) **Scale:** trained on trillions of tokens (vs. supervised tasks with millions of examples). (2) **Generality:** single model handles diverse tasks (translation, summarization, QA, code, reasoning) without task-specific architecture. (3) **Zero/few-shot:** works without task-specific training data (traditional NLP required 1000s of labeled examples). (4) **Emergent abilities:** exhibit reasoning, instruction following, in-context learning not explicitly trained for. (5) **Training paradigm:** unsupervised pretraining (self-supervised objective) rather than supervised fine-tuning. Traditional NLP (BERT, SVM classifiers) required task-specific labeled data; LLMs enable task generalization via pretraining + prompting.

**Interview Tip:** Emphasize the self-supervised training objective (next-token prediction) as foundational. Highlight the shift from task-specific supervised to general unsupervised pretraining. Discuss emergent abilities concretely.

### Q2: Explain scaling laws for LLMs. What are Chinchilla laws and why do they matter?

**A:** Scaling laws quantify how LLM loss decreases with model size N (parameters) and dataset size D (tokens). Empirical findings: `Loss(N) = A*N^{-α}` with α ≈ 0.07, `Loss(D) = B*D^{-β}` with β ≈ 0.2. Both matter: loss doesn't converge (more parameters help even with infinite data, more data helps even with infinite parameters). **Chinchilla laws** (Hoffman et al., 2022) provide optimal allocation: for fixed compute budget C, split equally between parameters and tokens. Concretely: train `N` parameter model on approximately `N*20` tokens. Example: 70B model trained on 1.4T tokens. Why matter: (1) **Predictability:** estimate model performance (loss) before training, enabling resource planning. (2) **Efficiency:** avoid under/over-allocating compute (e.g., training 100B model on 100B tokens wastes compute on parameters unused at plateau). (3) **Scaling strategy:** dictates how to spend additional compute (2x compute → train 2x larger model on 2x more data, not 4x larger model on same data). (4) **Emergent abilities:** scaling correlates with new capabilities (reasoning, few-shot learning emerge around 10B+ parameters, improving exponentially with scale). Practical implications: (1) Model size decision depends on available compute. (2) Data collection is as important as parameter count (scaling data without parameters hits ceiling). (3) Scaling laws hold across architectures, domains, languages (transferable). Caveats: Laws fit to observed data, may break at extreme scales; downstream performance sometimes decouples from loss (better loss ≠ always better downstream accuracy).

**Interview Tip:** Explain the power law formula and optimal allocation concretely (N ≈ D/20). Discuss why both matter (neither saturates). Mention that empirical scaling laws enable prediction without training.

### Q3: What are emergent abilities? Give examples and explain why they're surprising.

**A:** Emergent abilities are capabilities that appear only at large scales, absent or negligible in smaller models. Examples: (1) **Few-shot learning:** small model (1-2B params) can't learn from examples in prompt; 175B model (GPT-3) learns from 1-5 in-context examples without fine-tuning. (2) **Chain-of-thought reasoning:** small model outputs incoherent steps; large model with "let's think step by step" prefix solves problems. (3) **Instruction following:** "Write a poem about X" works well at scale, not at 1B. (4) **Code generation:** 1B model produces mostly wrong code; 100B model often correct (benchmarks: HumanEval F1 improves from 0% → 80%+ as scale increases). (5) **Reasoning:** math word problems, logical deduction solved at scale, not smaller. Why surprising: these abilities don't follow smoothly from loss scaling—loss improves monotonically with scale, but accuracy on downstream tasks exhibits phase transitions. E.g., few-shot learning has a threshold: models below ~100B can barely do it, at ~100B shows ~50% performance, at 500B+ nearly 100%. Interpretation: unclear whether emergent abilities are genuine phase transitions or artifacts of how we measure them. One theory: abilities exist in smaller models but are latent (optimization landscape makes them hard to access without scale); another: scale genuinely enables new reasoning patterns. Practical impact: these emergent abilities motivate continued scaling (solving new tasks without more labeled data). However, scale doesn't guarantee solving hard reasoning; even GPT-3 struggles with out-of-distribution examples.

**Interview Tip:** Give concrete few-shot learning example (e.g., "Q: 2+2=4, 3+3=6, 5+5=?"). Discuss the controversy (real phase transition vs measurement artifact). Show this motivates scaling beyond just loss improvement.

### Q4: Explain in-context learning. How does it differ from fine-tuning?

**A:** **In-context learning (ICL):** providing examples in the prompt (context window) teaches the model to solve a task without gradient updates. Example: prompt the model with [Example 1 Q & A, Example 2 Q & A, Test Question], model generates answer. Few-shot ICL: 1-5 examples. Zero-shot ICL: no examples, just instruction ("Classify as positive/negative: ..."). **Fine-tuning:** updating model weights on task-specific labeled data via backprop. Differences: (1) **Mechanism:** ICL uses attention to in-context examples (implicit learning in forward pass); fine-tuning updates parameters (explicit learning). (2) **Data efficiency:** fine-tuning requires labeled data (1000s of examples); ICL works with few examples. (3) **Speed:** ICL inference same cost (process all examples), fine-tuning enables faster inference (no examples needed). (4) **Generalization:** ICL generalizes to new examples within prompt; fine-tuning generalizes to new data. (5) **Interpretability:** ICL mechanisms visible (attention weights show which examples were used); fine-tuning is black-box. How ICL works: Transformer attends to context examples, pattern-matches (token "positive" in example → predict output "sentiment_positive"), learns task implicitly. At large scales, models develop in-context learning ability—small models show minimal ICL. Evidence: probing reveals models form task-specific representations from context (embeddings cluster based on task). Trade-off: ICL fills context window (if 10 examples per task, only ~100 examples fit in 4K context), fine-tuning requires compute/data but is more parameter-efficient.

**Interview Tip:** Explain the mechanism (attention to examples) vs fine-tuning (weight update). Show practical trade-off: ICL for few examples + immediate use, fine-tuning for many examples + repeated use. Mention scaling threshold for ICL.

### Q5: Explain instruction tuning. How is it different from supervised fine-tuning on task-specific data?

**A:** **Instruction tuning:** fine-tuning pretrained LLM on large collection of tasks with explicit instructions. Example: task = "summarize", instruction = "Summarize the following text in one sentence:", followed by text and summary. Data format: (instruction, input, output). Differs from traditional supervised fine-tuning: (1) **Multi-task:** fine-tune on hundreds of tasks simultaneously (vs. single task), enabling generalization to unseen tasks. (2) **Diverse formats:** tasks span classification, generation, extraction, reasoning. (3) **Instruction diversity:** same task (translation) phrased many ways ("Translate to French:", "French translation:", "Rephrase in French:"), teaching robustness. (4) **Scale:** instruction tuning datasets are large (millions of examples across tasks), teaching models to follow instructions reliably. Impact: massive—instruction-tuned models (Alpaca, Claude, GPT-3.5-turbo) are dramatically more usable than base LLMs (which ramble, struggle with instruction following). Mechanism: models learn a meta-task (understand instructions and follow them), enabling zero-shot generalization to new instructions not in training. Modern practice: base model (GPT-3) + instruction tuning (ChatGPT's recipe) + alignment (RLHF) produces usable assistant. Instruction tuning datasets: some from humans (expensive, high-quality), some from synthetic (GPT-4 generates data, cheaper but risk of distribution shift). Quality crucial: instructions must be clear, outputs must be correct. Mistakes propagate (if instruction dataset has errors, model learns error distribution).

**Interview Tip:** Explain the meta-learning aspect (learn to follow instructions on diverse tasks). Discuss why it's crucial for usable LLMs. Mention data quality importance—GIGO (garbage in, garbage out).

### Q6: Explain RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback). Why is it important for LLMs?

**A:** **RLHF:** align LLM outputs with human preferences via reinforcement learning. Pipeline: (1) Collect human preferences: show human raters two LLM outputs, ask which is better (more helpful, honest, harmless). Collect thousands of comparisons. (2) Train reward model: train supervised classifier to predict which output humans prefer. Inputs: (prompt, response); output: preference score (0-1). (3) RL fine-tuning: use reward model as reward signal in RL. Optimize `Σ reward(response) - β * KL(model || base_model)` where KL term prevents deviating too far from base model (maintains knowledge). Concretely: use PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) to update LLM to maximize expected reward. Why important: (1) **Alignment:** helps align model outputs with human values (helpful, harmless, honest). (2) **Quality:** human feedback captures nuances (tone, factuality, coherence) not captured by loss alone. (3) **Safety:** identifies harmful outputs and down-weights them. (4) **Practicality:** transforms base LLM (which can be rambling, incoherent) into usable assistant. Impact: RLHF is key ingredient in ChatGPT, Claude, GPT-4. Without it, models are less reliable and harder to use. Limitations: (1) Reward model can be biased (reflects annotator preferences, which vary). (2) Feedback collection expensive (human raters ~$0.50-2 per rating, millions needed). (3) Possible gaming: model learns to fool reward model (reward hacking) rather than genuinely improve. (4) KL penalty necessary but reduces optimization (model can't diverge far from base, limiting improvement). Modern alternatives: DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) skips reward model training, directly optimizes likelihood ratio; constitutional AI uses model-generated critique instead of human feedback.

**Interview Tip:** Explain the three-stage pipeline clearly. Discuss why reward model matters (captures human preference). Mention reward hacking risk and KL regularization as safeguard.

### Q7: Explain DPO (Direct Preference Optimization). How is it an improvement over RLHF?

**A:** **DPO:** directly optimize LLM based on human preferences without explicitly training a reward model. Motivation: RLHF requires two stages (train reward model, then RL fine-tuning), adding complexity and cost. DPO combines into one. Algorithm: given pairs of responses (preferred, dispreferred) for each prompt, directly optimize: `L = -E[(log σ(β * log(π(y_win|x) / π_ref(y_win|x)))) + log(1 - σ(β * log(π(y_loss|x) / π_ref(y_loss|x))))]` where σ is sigmoid, β is scaling factor (inverse temperature), π is model, π_ref is reference (base) model. Intuition: compare log-likelihood ratio of preferred vs dispreferred response under current model and base model. Maximize likelihood of preferred (numerator high) and minimize likelihood of dispreferred (numerator low). Advantages over RLHF: (1) **Simpler:** one-stage optimization (no reward model). (2) **Cheaper:** no human rating of individual outputs, just pairwise comparison (natural in preference data collection). (3) **More stable:** DPO directly optimizes human preference rather than proxy reward signal. (4) **Faster:** fewer training steps (no reward model training). Results: DPO matches or beats RLHF on standard benchmarks (AlpacaEval, MT-bench) with same human feedback data. Tradeoff: DPO is less flexible (requires pairwise comparisons, not arbitrary rewards). Implementation: relatively simple (one-liner PyTorch loss), enabling wider adoption. Modern practice: DPO increasingly preferred over RLHF due to simplicity and empirical equivalence. Variants: IPO (Identity Preference Optimization), ORPO (Odds Ratio Preference Optimization) further improve.

**Interview Tip:** Explain how DPO avoids reward model by directly comparing preference pairs. Show the loss formula (interpret sigmoid as comparing log-likelihood ratios). Mention empirical equivalence to RLHF but simpler.

### Q8: Explain Constitutional AI. How does it address safety and alignment?

**A:** **Constitutional AI:** align LLM with human values using a set of principles (constitution) and model self-criticism. Approach: (1) Define constitution: set of principles (e.g., "Be helpful", "Be harmless", "Be honest", "Be harmless to illegal activity"). (2) Red-teaming: have the model generate potentially harmful outputs (via adversarial prompts or by disabling safety mechanisms). (3) Self-critique: prompt model to critique its own outputs against the constitution. Example: "Given the constitution [list], critique this response: [harmful output]". (4) Supervised learning: collect (input, harmful output, critique, revised output) tuples, fine-tune to follow critiques. (5) RL (optional): use critiques as implicit reward signal, optimize model to generate self-improved outputs. Why effective: (1) Reduces reliance on human feedback (model self-improves). (2) Constitutional principles are explicit and auditable (vs. reward model which is black-box). (3) Enables fine-grained control (update constitution to change behavior). (4) Generalizes better (model learns to apply principles to new domains). Example: if constitution says "Don't provide instructions for illegal activity", model should refuse novel illegal requests without explicit training. Limitations: (1) Constitution quality matters (bad principles → bad outputs). (2) Model can still be fooled (jailbreaking via creative prompts). (3) Tension between helpfulness and harmlessness (being maximally harmless makes system unhelpful). Comparison to RLHF: RLHF uses human feedback on outcomes; constitutional AI uses model-generated feedback on principles. Constitutional AI is cheaper (no human raters) and potentially more principled. Modern impact: Claude's approach uses constitutional AI + limited RLHF, balancing automation and human oversight.

**Interview Tip:** Explain self-critique mechanism (model critiques own outputs). Discuss explicitness of constitution (auditable vs reward model black-box). Mention the helpfulness-harmlessness tradeoff.

### Q9: What is the hallucination problem in LLMs? Why does it occur and how do we mitigate it?

**A:** **Hallucination:** LLM generates plausible-sounding but factually incorrect or fabricated information. Examples: citing nonexistent papers with plausible titles, reporting false statistics, inventing dialog. Why it occurs: (1) **Probabilistic model:** LLM samples from learned distribution, not retrieves from knowledge base. If training distribution includes plausible-seeming falsehoods or if model hasn't seen a fact, it may generate a guess. (2) **Autoregressive generation:** at each token, model predicts most likely next token given context. If uncertainty high, model may pick a token that sounds good but leads to hallucination. (3) **Scaling property:** larger models hallucinate more (more capacity to invent details). (4) **Task distribution:** some tasks inherently more prone (generation > retrieval). Mitigations: (1) **Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG):** augment model with retrieved context from documents. Answer questions based on retrieved passages (reduces dependence on memorized knowledge). (2) **Fine-tuning on factuality:** instruction-tune on dataset emphasizing accurate responses. (3) **Few-shot prompting:** provide examples of accurate answers in context. (4) **Prompt engineering:** "Answer based on the following passage:", explicit instructions reduce hallucination. (5) **Uncertainty quantification:** output confidence/credibility score alongside answer. (6) **Fact-checking module:** run generated output through fact-checker (NER + knowledge base lookup). (7) **Constitutional AI:** teach model to be "honest" about uncertainty ("I don't know" acceptable). (8) **Ensemble:** aggregate multiple model outputs, vote on facts. Fundamental challenge: models excel at statistical patterns, not symbolic reasoning about facts. Even with mitigations, hallucinations remain non-zero. Best approach: context-specific (RAG for Q&A, constitutional AI for open generation, fact-checking for critical applications).

**Interview Tip:** Explain the probabilistic model perspective (sampling vs retrieval). Discuss why scaling increases hallucination. Emphasize RAG as practical mitigation. Mention the fundamental tradeoff (fluency vs factuality).

### Q10: Explain temperature and top-k/top-p (nucleus) sampling. How do they affect generation quality?

**A:** LLMs generate text by sampling from predicted probability distribution at each token. Temperature (T) and sampling strategy control output randomness/diversity. **Temperature:** scales logits before softmax. `p_i = softmax(logits_i / T)`. Low T (0.1): sharper distribution (high-prob tokens dominate), more deterministic. T=1: original distribution. High T (2.0): flatter distribution (all tokens more likely), more random. Mathematical: at T→0, softmax → argmax (greedy decoding, always pick highest prob). At T→∞, softmax → uniform (random). Quality tradeoff: low T = focused, coherent output but boring, repetitive; high T = creative but incoherent. **Top-K sampling:** sample from top-k highest probability tokens, ignore rest. Reduces nonsensical low-probability tokens. E.g., k=50 keeps only 50 most likely tokens. **Top-P (nucleus) sampling:** sample from smallest set of tokens with cumulative probability ≥ p. E.g., p=0.9 includes tokens until 90% probability mass covered. Advantage: adaptive to distribution (if top token is 95% likely, takes 1 token; if top token is 30%, takes more). Modern recommendation: top-p with p=0.9 (or 0.95) + temperature 0.7-1.0 balances quality and diversity. Examples: low T (0.3) for factual Q&A, high T (1.2) for creative writing. Beam search (decode multiple hypotheses, keep best) is alternative to sampling, deterministic and often higher quality but slower. Why this matters: LLM quality heavily depends on sampling strategy (same model, different T → very different output). Production systems tune T per use-case (strict Q&A: T=0.3, creative: T=0.9).

**Interview Tip:** Explain temperature as logit scaling with concrete examples. Discuss top-p's advantage over top-k (adaptive). Mention use-case-specific tuning as best practice.

### Q11: Explain chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting and why it improves reasoning.

**A:** **Chain-of-thought prompting:** prepend "Let's think step by step" or similar to prompt, asking model to show reasoning before answering. Example: instead of "Q: 2+2+2=?", use "Q: 2+2+2=? Let's think step by step. Step 1: 2+2=4. Step 2: 4+2=6. Answer: 6." Results: dramatically improves accuracy on reasoning tasks. Benchmark improvements: 58% → 78% accuracy on GSM8K (grade school math word problems, GPT-3). Why effective: (1) **Intermediate steps:** breaking problem into steps reduces cognitive load on model (easier to get each step right than leap to answer). (2) **Error correction:** intermediate steps reveal reasoning errors (if step 1 is wrong, model catches it before answer). (3) **Gradient flow:** during fine-tuning, loss on intermediate steps helps learning (more supervision signal). (4) **Emergence:** CoT emerges with scale (small models don't benefit much; 100B+ models benefit greatly). Mechanism: unclear but theories include: (a) Intermediate text provides implicit training signal (model learns to check work). (b) Tokens allocated to reasoning rather than premature conclusion. (c) Larger models have capacity for planning behavior. Variants: (1) **Few-shot CoT:** provide examples with reasoning steps, model imitates. (2) **Self-consistency:** generate k reasoning paths, vote on answers (ensemble of chains). (3) **Tree of thought:** expand multiple reasoning branches, search for best path. Limitations: CoT helps on math/logic, less on retrieval/classification. Also, reasoning can be wrong (model shows steps but steps are illogical or incorrect). Modern practice: prompt engineering heavily uses CoT; fine-tuning on CoT data improves further. Some debate whether CoT teaches reasoning or just enables model to show work (is reasoning present in base model, just not shown?).

**Interview Tip:** Show concrete example (math problem solved step-by-step). Explain why breaking into steps helps (reduced cognitive load). Mention scaling threshold (emerges at large scales). Discuss some reasoning is illusory (steps are plausible-sounding but wrong).

### Q12: What are LLM evaluation benchmarks? Explain MMLU, HumanEval, HellaSwag.

**A:** LLM evaluation requires diverse benchmarks (task distribution, difficulty, format). Key benchmarks: (1) **MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding):** 15K multiple-choice questions spanning 57 diverse subjects (history, STEM, law, medicine). Measures knowledge breadth. GPT-3: 65% (below random on many subjects), GPT-3.5: 70%, GPT-4: 86%. Performance gap between models is substantial. (2) **HumanEval:** 164 Python programming problems. Model writes code to solve specification, graded by automated test suite. F1 score: fraction of problems solved (code runs correctly). GPT-3: 28.8%, GPT-4: 67.0%, human engineers: 80%. Measures reasoning and code knowledge. (3) **HellaSwag:** 70K multiple-choice questions about videos (given partial video, choose next event from 4 options). Measures commonsense reasoning. Humans: 95%, GPT-3: 79%, GPT-4: 95%+. Other standard benchmarks: (4) **SQuAD:** reading comprehension (answer questions based on passage). (5) **SuperGLUE:** diverse NLU tasks (NLI, QA, coreference, paraphrase detection). (6) **BLEU/ROUGE:** MT/summarization (automatic metric, imperfect). (7) **TruthfulQA:** measure hallucination (does model answer truthfully?). (8) **AlpacaEval, MT-bench:** human-evaluated instruction-following (expensive but high-signal). Limitations: (1) Benchmark contamination (models may have memorized test data from pretraining). (2) Gaming (models optimized for specific benchmarks, don't generalize). (3) Instability (small seed changes can swing scores). (4) Misalignment with real-world performance (high MMLU doesn't guarantee usefulness for users). Best practice: diverse benchmarks (not relying on single score) + human evaluation for critical tasks.

**Interview Tip:** Describe each benchmark's purpose concretely. Mention benchmark saturation (as models improve, benchmark becomes less informative). Discuss why multiple benchmarks essential (no single metric sufficient).

### Q13: What factors affect LLM tokenomics and cost? How do they impact deployment?

**A:** Tokenomics refers to costs of using LLMs (training + inference). **Training cost:** scales as compute = C ∝ N*D (parameters × tokens). Example: training GPT-3 (175B params, 300B tokens) estimated at $1-10M. Cost multiplied by multiple passes (validation, ablations). Factors: (1) GPU/TPU cost (~$1-5 per GPU-hour), (2) Infrastructure overhead (cooling, power), (3) Data collection. **Inference cost:** scales with sequence length. (1) **Per-token cost:** forward pass through transformer is O(seqlen × hidden_dim × num_layers). Long sequences are more expensive. (2) **KV-cache memory:** O(seqlen × num_layers × hidden_dim); at 4K context, 70B model requires ~1TB memory (needs multiple GPUs). (3) **Latency vs throughput:** batch size affects (larger batches, lower per-token cost but higher latency). Deployment impacts: (1) **Model size:** smaller models cheaper to serve (7B cheaper than 70B). (2) **Quantization:** reduce precision (fp32 → int8), 4x memory reduction, faster inference, minimal accuracy loss. (3) **Batching:** multiple requests processed together reduces per-request cost. (4) **Rate limiting:** cap requests/second to manage cost. (5) **On-device inference:** run small models locally (eliminate API calls). (6) **Caching:** cache popular queries (identical requests reuse cached output). **Cost comparison:** OpenAI GPT-3.5: ~$0.0005/1K tokens, GPT-4: ~$0.01-0.03/1K tokens (100x more expensive). Open-source LLaMA: free inference on own hardware. Practical implications: large models economically viable only at scale (millions of queries); startups often use smaller open-source models or API. Trade-off: GPT-4 quality vs LLaMA cost.

**Interview Tip:** Explain the O(N*D) training cost and O(seqlen) inference cost. Discuss quantization as practical optimization. Mention why deployment choices matter (cost-quality tradeoff).

### Q14: Explain context window and long-context models. Why is extending context challenging?

**A:** **Context window:** maximum sequence length input to LLM. Standard: 2K (GPT-2), 4K (GPT-3), 8K (GPT-4), 100K+ (Claude, GPT-4-turbo). Larger context enables: (1) Longer documents (multi-page PDFs, full codebases). (2) More examples (few-shot in-context learning). (3) Longer conversations. Challenges extending context: (1) **Compute:** attention is O(seqlen^2), 10x longer sequence → 100x compute. Flash attention mitigates but doesn't eliminate. (2) **Memory:** KV-cache is O(seqlen); 100K context at 70B model → hundreds of GB memory. (3) **Positional encoding:** most position encodings (absolute, even RoPE) trained on fixed max length, extrapolation to longer sequences hurts. (4) **Lost in the middle:** information in middle of long context has lower attention (found empirically—models attend to beginning and end, ignore middle). Solutions: (1) **Sparse/local attention:** limit attention to nearby tokens, reduce O(seqlen^2) → O(seqlen * window_size). (2) **Hierarchical compression:** summarize chunks, attend to summaries. (3) **Retrieval-augmented generation:** don't pass full context, retrieve relevant passages instead. (4) **Relative positional encodings:** RoPE enables better extrapolation than absolute. (5) **Long-context pretraining:** continue training on sequences longer than 4K. Modern LLMs (Claude 3, GPT-4-turbo) extend to 100K via combination: training on longer sequences + architectural improvements (flash attention) + KV-cache optimization. Remaining challenges: (1) Lost-in-the-middle persists (mid-context tokens attended less). (2) Cost still high (100K tokens → more compute/memory than 4K). (3) Quality degrades slightly (models don't leverage full context effectively on all tasks).

**Interview Tip:** Explain the O(seqlen^2) bottleneck motivating sparse attention. Mention lost-in-the-middle as interesting empirical finding. Discuss RAG as alternative to extending context directly.

### Q15: Explain mixture of experts (MoE) models. Why are they useful for scaling LLMs?

**A:** **Mixture of Experts (MoE):** neural architecture where each layer has multiple expert networks (specialized sub-models), router dynamically routes input to subset of experts. Example: 64 experts, router selects top-2 per token. Architecture: `output = Σ_i gate(token)_i * expert_i(token)` where gate is trainable router (e.g., softmax over expert scores). Only selected experts compute (sparse activation). Why useful: (1) **Parameter efficiency:** with 64 experts, model has 64x more parameters but only uses ~3% per token (sparse). 1.6T parameter MoE equivalent to 100B dense model in compute. (2) **Specialization:** different experts learn different skills (syntax, semantics, arithmetic), improving expressiveness. (3) **Throughput:** sparse computation enables higher throughput (same compute, more parameters). Challenges: (1) **Training instability:** router can collapse (all tokens routed to few experts). Techniques: auxiliary loss encouraging even routing, sticky routing (once assigned to expert, stay). (2) **Load balancing:** uneven expert utilization (some experts overused, others idle). (3) **Communication cost:** gathering outputs from multiple GPUs (if experts distributed) adds overhead. (4) **Fine-tuning:** expert routing difficult to adapt to new domains (expert utilization may shift). Examples: Switch Transformer (sparse T5 variant), GLaM (Google), Mixtral 8x7B (better than 57B dense). Results: MoE models competitive with dense models on benchmarks while using 2-4x fewer FLOPs. Modern trend: MoE increasingly popular for large-scale LLMs due to training efficiency. However, inference cost still high (activating multiple experts even if routing sparse).

**Interview Tip:** Explain the sparse activation concept (compute only top-k experts). Discuss parameter efficiency: 1.6T parameters, 100B compute similar to 100B dense model. Mention training instability and solutions (auxiliary loss).

## Interview Cheatsheet

**Key Terms:**
- **LLM:** Transformer-based model trained via next-token prediction on massive text, enabling few-shot and zero-shot generalization
- **Scaling Laws:** Loss decreases as power law with model size (N) and data (D); Chinchilla allocation: N ≈ D/20
- **Emergent Abilities:** Qualitatively new capabilities (few-shot, reasoning) appearing at large scales (100B+), not present in smaller models
- **In-Context Learning:** Teaching model via examples in prompt, without gradient updates; enables few-shot without fine-tuning
- **Instruction Tuning:** Fine-tuning on diverse tasks with explicit instructions, enabling zero-shot generalization to unseen tasks
- **RLHF:** Collect human preferences, train reward model, use RL to maximize reward; aligns outputs with human values
- **DPO:** Direct preference optimization; simpler than RLHF, directly optimizes on preference pairs without reward model
- **Chain-of-Thought:** Prompt model to show reasoning steps before answer; dramatically improves accuracy on reasoning tasks
- **Hallucination:** Model generates plausible but false information; mitigated via RAG, fine-tuning, fact-checking
- **Temperature:** Scales logits before softmax; low T (0-0.5) = deterministic, high T (1-2) = diverse
- **Context Window:** Max sequence length; limited by O(seqlen^2) attention and O(seqlen) KV-cache memory
- **MoE (Mixture of Experts):** Sparse model with multiple expert networks, router selects subset; more parameters, same compute as dense

**Rapid-Fire Q&A:**
- **Q: LLM vs traditional NLP?** **A:** LLM: unsupervised pretraining on billions tokens, generalizes to diverse tasks; traditional: supervised on task-specific data
- **Q: Scaling law formula?** **A:** Loss ∝ N^{-α} with α ≈ 0.07; allocate compute equally to parameters and tokens (Chinchilla)
- **Q: Why few-shot learning emerges at scale?** **A:** Unknown; theories include pattern-matching from context examples, larger capacity for meta-learning
- **Q: Instruction tuning vs fine-tuning?** **A:** Instruction tuning: multi-task, diverse instructions, teaches generalization; fine-tuning: single task, more parameters updated
- **Q: Why RLHF important?** **A:** Aligns outputs with human preferences (helpful, harmless, honest); transforms base LLM into usable assistant
- **Q: DPO advantage?** **A:** Simpler than RLHF (one-stage), cheaper (no reward model), directly optimizes preferences
- **Q: Chain-of-thought mechanism?** **A:** Intermediate steps reduce cognitive load, enable error correction, allocate tokens to reasoning not premature conclusion
- **Q: Hallucination cause?** **A:** Probabilistic model samples plausible guesses when uncertain; mitigated via RAG, fact-checking, constitutional AI
- **Q: Temperature effect?** **A:** Low T (0.3): focused, deterministic; high T (1.5): creative, random; T=1 is default distribution
- **Q: Why extend context hard?** **A:** O(seqlen^2) attention, O(seqlen) KV-cache; lost-in-the-middle problem; mitigated by sparse attention, RAG
